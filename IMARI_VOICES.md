# Imari Voices — Website

> **Repo:** Website (`imari-website`) version. The sibling version lives at `IMARI_VOICES.md` in the `imari-house-manual` repo. The voices themselves (Host Voice, banned words, the verb diet) are identical across both repos; application notes differ — the website uses Host Voice for persuasive body copy, where the manual uses it for instructions.

> **Precedence:** This file is the voice authority. `BRAND.md` covers visual identity and quick-reference voice rules; for depth on voice — named voices, smell tests, the verb diet — this file wins.

A growing reference for the tones we write in. Each entry names a voice,
describes when to use it, and gives the rules and examples that make it
recognizable.

This file is meant to grow. Add new voices as we identify them. Refine
existing ones as we use them in more contexts. The point is to make tone
**portable**: any writer (human or model) should be able to pick a voice
from this file and produce copy that sounds like Imari without having to
re-derive the rules from scratch.

---

## How to use this file

When briefing collateral, name the voice. Examples:

- "Write the calendar cards in **host voice**."
- "The hero headline is in **host voice**, the close is more reflective."
- "This is for the website — use **host voice** unless I tell you otherwise."

When reviewing draft copy, diagnose with the voice's smell tests. If a
sentence fails them, rewrite or cut.

---

## Universal rules (apply across every voice)

These constraints sit above the voices. A piece can be in any voice and
still has to follow these.

**Banned words:**
- bespoke
- curated
- world-class
- unparalleled

**Capped words:**
- "luxury" — twice per piece, ideally zero. "Comfortable luxury" is the
  brand's own ethos line and is the one sanctioned place for it.

**Banned punctuation:**
- exclamation marks

**Sparingly used:**
- em-dashes — allowed for rhythm only, never as a substitute for a period or where a comma will do. The brand uses them on imari.cc; the test is whether the sentence breathes better with one. If it does, keep it. If not, cut it.

**Banned phrasing patterns:**
- "address" used as a noun for the property ("the address you stay at
  when..."). Use *place, estate, residence, private acre.* Street
  addresses are fine.
- Filler taglines that sound profound but say nothing. ("The address
  you stay at when the address matters.") If a line could appear on
  any luxury brand, it shouldn't appear on ours.
- Salesy lofty register. ("We hold the table, the driver waits.")
  Imari is secure in its offering and lets the offering speak.
- **Marketing CTA phrases on collateral.** ("Inquire now. Reserve. Book today.")
  The email closes; the document doesn't.
- **Functional CTAs** in operational contexts (house manual, instructional
  content) are different — *"Ask the concierge,"* *"Call extension 0,"*
  *"Message us"* are operational instructions, not sales asks, and are
  allowed. Lean declarative by default ("The concierge handles dietary
  preferences" over "Ask the concierge about dietary preferences"); reserve
  imperatives for moments that genuinely need them — urgency, safety, or
  numbered instruction steps.

**Sanctioned framings (use freely when relevant):**
- "to host, to hold court, to headquarter" — the three-verb phrase
  that describes what Imari uniquely offers.
- "Comfortable luxury" — brand ethos.
- "Less than two miles from every event on the program" — the
  proximity tagline; always italic Cormorant, stands alone.
- The four pillars: *Entirely Yours, Expansive, Base of Operations,
  Completely Secure.*

# The Voices

---

## Host Voice

**One-line:** The narrator is the host of the house, briefing the
principal. Competent, unshowy, on top of things.

**When to use it.** Default voice for almost all Imari collateral. Event
cards, possibilities lists, weekend agendas, room descriptions, anything
where you're explaining what's available or what's been arranged.

On the website, Host Voice carries the bulk of body copy — property and
service descriptions, offering particulars, weekend agendas. Reflective
Voice is reserved for hero taglines and closing lines; Argument Voice
handles the persuasive positioning paragraphs. The sibling
`IMARI_VOICES.md` in `imari-house-manual` extends Host Voice further to
operational and instructional content — the chief of staff explaining
how the house works.

**When not to use it.** Brand poetry on the home page hero. The
proximity tagline. Anywhere the writing is supposed to feel reflective
or aspirational rather than operational.

**The role.** The narrator is the chief of staff briefing the
principal. They are not a marketer, not a screenwriter, not a friend.
They know the house, they've made the arrangements, and they are telling
the principal what is ready.

**The five rules.**

1. **State what's there. Let the reader imagine the rest.** The
   persuasion is in the facts, not in the framing.
2. **No mood-setting before the content.** No "as the city quiets,"
   no "imagine," no "picture this." Open with what's true.
3. **Concrete verbs only.** *Poured, cut, waiting, ready, on the
   clock.* Cut: *elevate, experience, savor, indulge, transform, enjoy.*
4. **End sentences on a noun or a real condition.** Not on a flourish.
5. **Specifics over scale.** *Two hundred guests* beats *unforgettable
   evening.* *A Michelin chef on the grill* beats *culinary excellence.*

**The diagnostic question.** Could a chief of staff say this with a
straight face while briefing the CEO? If yes, it's host voice. If it
sounds like a brochure, magazine prose, or a screenplay, it isn't.

**Smell tests.** Any one of these is a red flag:

- The sentence describes a feeling instead of a thing.
- The narrator is pointing at the moment instead of delivering it.
- There's a metaphor doing work that a noun could do.
- A clause exists to set tone rather than deliver content.
- The reader is told how to feel.

**Examples.**

GOOD (host voice):
> Your guests arrive home to drinks poured and cigars cut in the
> garden. A late supper waiting if anyone is hungry, and the staff on
> the clock as long as the conversation runs.

BAD (cinematic voice — pointing at the moment):
> The party walks back through the gates as the city quiets. Cigars
> in the garden, an old fashioned at the bar, supper in the kitchen
> for anyone still hungry.

GOOD (host voice):
> A viewing party for up to two hundred guests. Big screens in the
> yard, the ballroom, and poolside. A Michelin chef on the grill.
> Drinks passed all night. The last guests walk upstairs and call it.

BAD (marketing voice — telling the reader how to feel):
> An unforgettable evening of curated luxury, with screens
> throughout the estate and the finest cuisine, designed to make
> every moment count.

GOOD (host voice):
> A pre-game at the residence before the gates open. Drinks, hors
> d'oeuvres, and an unhurried hour on the lawn. A late dinner ready
> for everyone who comes back.

BAD (lede-and-flourish voice):
> Come back to the garden lit. Pre-game from the lawn, walk down to
> the show. Hors d'oeuvres and cocktails before the gates open.

**Quick reference for the "verb diet."**

Use freely:
*arrive · wait · pour · cut · pass · ready · on the clock · open ·
close · run · walk · come back · turn in · stand by · plan · book ·
host · hold · keep*

Use sparingly or not at all:
*elevate · curate · craft · transform · indulge · savor · experience
· enjoy · unwind · escape · discover · embark · immerse · enchant ·
captivate · delight · inspire*

# Voices to add later

A running list of tones we've used or might want, so we don't lose them.
When we use one consistently enough to define it, promote it up into the
file.

- **Reflective voice** — for closes, taglines, and the rare line that
  is meant to land emotionally. The proximity tagline ("Imari Georgetown
  is less than two miles from every event on the program") is in this
  voice. Italic Cormorant, declarative, stands alone. To document
  properly: when is reflective right? How is it different from poetry?
  What's the limit on it per piece?

- **Argument voice** — for the persuasive paragraphs. The opportunity
  section on the UFC page is in this voice. It is more emphatic than host
  voice and willing to make a claim, but still in service of facts. Rules
  to capture: how does it stay this side of salesy? What's the relation
  to host voice (probably: same narrator, different register).

- **Catalog voice** — for the four-pillar value grid descriptions.
  Currently labeled and factual. To document: parallel structure,
  noun-led, no verbs of feeling, length cap per cell.
